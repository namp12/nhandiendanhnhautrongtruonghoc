Abstract—Early detection of school violence is critical for
student safety but remains challenging under real-time surveil-
lance and resource constraints. While recent deep learning
approaches achieve high recognition accuracy, most studies
overlook deployment feasibility in continuous monitoring sce-
narios. This work presents a deployment-oriented benchmark
of lightweight video models for real-time school violence de-
tection, with a focus on streaming-based architectures. We
systematically evaluate representative spatiotemporal models,
including MoViNet streaming variants, under a unified training
and evaluation protocol. To enhance contextual relevance, we
introduce RWF-2000+, an extension of the RWF-2000 dataset
incorporating school-oriented surveillance scenarios. Beyond
accuracy, we conduct edge-device evaluation on an NVIDIA
Jetson AGX Orin, analyzing latency, throughput, and mixed-
precision optimization. Results show that lightweight stream-
ing models achieve a favorable accuracy-efficiency trade-off,
enabling real-time on-device inference and highlighting the
importance of deployment-aware evaluation for practical school
surveillance systems.
Index Terms—School violence detection, Real-time video,
Lightweight models, Streaming networks, Jetson AGX Orin.
I. INTRODUCTION
School violence remains a critical societal issue that
directly threatens students’ physical safety and psychologi-
cal well-being, with long-term consequences for academic
performance, mental health, and social development [1].
Incidents such as physical aggression, bullying, or sudden
confrontations often occur without clear warning signs and
may escalate rapidly in crowded educational environments.
As a result, there is an increasing demand for intelligent
surveillance systems capable of early detection and timely
intervention to support safer school environments [2].
Conventional security practices based on manual mon-
itoring or rule-based alarm systems are often ineffective
in real-world school settings. Human operators are prone
to fatigue and subjective judgment, while rule-based ap-
proaches struggle to generalize across diverse interaction
patterns and environmental conditions [3]. Earlier computer
vision techniques relying on handcrafted features, such as
optical flow, background subtraction, or trajectory analysis,
also exhibit limited robustness under variations in view-
point, illumination, occlusion, and scene complexity, which
restricts their scalability in practical deployments [4].
Recent advances in deep learning have significantly im-
proved automatic violence detection by enabling models
to learn spatiotemporal representations directly from raw
video data. Architectures such as 3D Convolutional Neural
Networks (3D CNNs) and CNN–LSTM hybrids have demon-
strated promising performance in capturing motion dynamics
associated with violent behavior [5], [6]. However, these
models are typically computationally intensive, requiring
substantial processing power and memory, which makes
them difficult to deploy in real-time school surveillance
systems operating under limited hardware and latency con-
straints.
Despite these advances, many existing studies continue
to prioritize recognition accuracy as the primary objective,
with comparatively less attention given to computational
efficiency and deployment feasibility. Moreover, commonly
used benchmarks such as RWF-2000, while effective for
evaluating generic violence detection, do not fully cap-
ture the temporal characteristics and contextual diversity of
authentic school surveillance scenarios. These limitations
motivate the need for a more school-oriented benchmark
setting and a systematic evaluation that jointly considers
recognition performance and runtime efficiency under con-
sistent experimental conditions.
In this work, we investigate lightweight deep learning
models for real-time school violence detection, with a par-
ticular focus on streaming-based video recognition archi-
tectures. Instead of aiming solely for peak accuracy, we
emphasize the balance between detection performance and

The main contributions of this study are summarized as
follows:
• We construct RWF-2000+, an extended dataset that

II. RELATED WORKS
A. Video Violence Detection Datasets
Datasets serve as the foundational component for video-
based violence detection, determining both the generalization
capability and stability of deep learning models. Early bench-
mark datasets, such as the Hockey Fight Dataset, record
violent encounters during hockey games. However, their
scope and contextual diversity are limited, representing only
a narrow form of violence [7]. Other datasets, such as the
Movies Fight Dataset, increase diversity by extracting clips
from commercial films; nevertheless, their staged nature
makes it difficult for models to generalize effectively to
real-world surveillance scenarios. The RWF-2000 dataset,
introduced by Cheng et al. [8] has become a standard bench-
mark in this field. It consists of 2,000 real-world surveillance
videos labeled as “violent” or “non-violent,” significantly
improving representativeness and spatiotemporal learning
potential . Both Vijeikis et al. [7] and Torre [9] confirmed
that RWF-2000 remains one of the most suitable datasets
for training and evaluating deep learning models in non-
laboratory surveillance conditions. More recently, Kaur and
Singh [10] combined the RWF-2000 and RLVS datasets to
evaluate a transfer-learning-based ensemble model, demon-
strating strong generalization across diverse surveillance
contexts. Similarly, Khan et al. [11] conducted cross-domain
evaluations involving the RWF-2000, Surveillance Fight, and
Industrial Surveillance datasets, highlighting model robust-
ness and stability under varied monitoring environments.
However, most existing datasets still fail to capture suf-
ficient diversity in terms of temporal dynamics, illumi-
nation conditions, and contextual variations, particularly
within school environments. To bridge this gap, this study
introduces RWF-2000+, an extended version of the orig-
inal dataset designed to enhance data diversity, density,
and realism, thereby improving applicability to educational
surveillance scenarios.
B. Deep Learning Models for Violence Detection
The advancement of deep learning has enabled diverse
approaches for extracting spatiotemporal representations
in video-based violence recognition, ranging from hybrid
CNN–3D CNN pipelines to lightweight architectures de-
signed for real-time surveillance. Khan et al. [11] proposed a
three-stage framework that combines lightweight CNNs with
3D CNNs to jointly model spatial and temporal cues, and
their cross-domain evaluation reported stable performance
across multiple surveillance datasets. Kaur and Singh [10]
introduced a transfer-learning-based ensemble that integrates
three pretrained backbones with a stacked LSTM, achieving
strong results on both RLVS and RWF-2000. Along a similar
direction, Aung et al. [12] presented a dual-stream model that
fuses human pose and motion dynamics via weighted fusion,
improving recognition performance on RWF-2000.
Several works have further emphasized efficiency and
deployability. Zhu and Hu [13] proposed a lightweight two-
stream spatiotemporal network deployed in a collaborative
cloud–edge architecture to reduce computational cost while
maintaining competitive accuracy on RWF-2000. Wang et al.
[14] developed an EfficientNet-B0-based model augmented
with Bi-LTMA and TSM mechanisms to balance accuracy
and runtime efficiency for surveillance devices. D¨undar et
al. [15] proposed a shallow 3D CNN with only 54.6K
parameters, demonstrating that compact architectures can
achieve reasonable performance across RWF-2000, RLVS,
and Hockey Fight datasets.
Beyond RGB-based spatiotemporal CNNs, recent studies
have explored skeleton/graph representations to improve ro-
bustness under challenging surveillance conditions. Tran et
al. [16], [17] model skeleton sequences as spatiotemporal
graphs and leverage GCN-based designs (with attention
mechanisms) to capture joint dynamics for violence recog-
nition on benchmarks such as RWF-2000. Complementary
to algorithmic advances, system-oriented studies also em-
phasize practical deployment aspects; for example, Jeevan
and Avanthika [4] present an intelligent video surveillance
framework integrating real-time detection modules and post-
processing to reduce false alarms.
While recent deep learning–based approaches have re-
ported strong performance in violence detection, many rely
on computationally intensive or multi-stream architectures
that are difficult to deploy in real-time surveillance settings.
Even among studies that explicitly consider efficiency or
edge-awareness, evaluations are frequently conducted on
cloud GPUs or proxy platforms rather than under realistic

embedded constraints. As a result, a gap remains between
reported recognition performance and practical deployability,
particularly for school surveillance scenarios where low
latency, limited resources, and continuous operation are
critical.
III. EXTENDED VIOLENCE BEHAVIOR DATASET
Existing violence detection datasets, such as RWF-2000,
have been widely used to evaluate deep learning models;
however, their applicability to school surveillance scenarios
remains limited. Most videos were collected from public
spaces, resulting in differences in context, illumination, and
camera viewpoints compared with typical school environ-
ments. Moreover, the violent behaviors captured are often
large-scale and spontaneous, which do not fully reflect the
subtle and short-duration interactions commonly observed in
educational settings. These factors can hinder the effective
generalization of models in school-oriented surveillance ap-
plications.
A. Scientific Basis for Dataset Expansion
Although RWF-2000 is a widely used benchmark for
violence detection, its visual context and interaction patterns
differ from those typically observed in school surveillance.
Most clips are captured in outdoor or semi-public envi-
ronments (e.g., streets and commercial areas), resulting in
camera viewpoints, illumination, crowd density, and inter-
personal distances that may not transfer well to indoor
school settings. In contrast, school surveillance often involves
constrained spaces, more frequent close-range interactions,
and subtle physical contacts that can challenge violence
classifiers and increase false alarms.
To improve contextual relevance and support more reliable
evaluation in educational scenarios, we introduce an addi-
tional dataset, termed RWF-2000+. RWF-2000+ is designed
to (i) broaden viewpoint and illumination diversity represen-
tative of indoor school cameras, (ii) include school-like inter-
action patterns through carefully scripted, safety-supervised
enactments, and (iii) enable evaluation under conditions that
resemble continuous, real-time school monitoring (e.g., fixed
camera placement and typical indoor backgrounds).
The supplementary samples were collected in controlled
indoor school environments using camera placements consis-
tent with common classroom surveillance setups. Recordings
focus on mild physical interactions performed by student
volunteers under supervision to ensure safety and ethi-
cal compliance. While these staged scenarios cannot fully
capture the unpredictability of real incidents, they provide
additional school-oriented motion patterns and background
cues that complement the original RWF-2000. Therefore,
RWF-2000+ is intended to augment–not replace–RWF-2000
by offering a more school-relevant testbed with increased
contextual diversity.
B. Architecture and Organization of the RWF-2000+ Dataset
The expanded RWF-2000+ dataset contains approximately
2,540 short surveillance clips annotated into two classes,
Fight and Non-Fight. Each clip is recorded at 25 frames per
second (fps) with an average duration of 4–5 seconds, which
provides standardized segments for clip-level benchmarking
and captures short-term motion cues relevant to violent in-
teractions in practical surveillance settings. Importantly, this
duration does not represent the decision latency of streaming
models: MoViNet-Stream produces frame-progressive pre-
dictions and can, in principle, trigger an alert before the end
of the clip once sufficient evidence is observed. A dedicated
early-warning protocol to quantify the minimum observation
window for stable alerting (e.g., sub-second triggering time)
will be included in an extended version of this benchmark.
RWF-2000+ combines two complementary sources. The
first component is the original RWF-2000 benchmark, con-
sisting of 2,000 real-world surveillance videos collected from
public areas (e.g., streets, shopping malls, and train stations).
These clips typically depict spontaneous and unstructured
events and exhibit substantial variability in viewpoint, illu-
mination, and crowd density. The second component consists
of 540 supplementary clips newly recorded in controlled,
school-like indoor environments with camera viewpoints
consistent with classroom surveillance setups. These record-
ings focus on school-relevant interactions performed by
student volunteers under supervision to ensure safety and
ethical compliance.
In the supplementary RWF-2000+ recordings, the Non-
Fight class focuses on close-proximity but non-violent in-
teractions that frequently occur in school environments,
including handshakes, conversations at short interpersonal
distance, and mild incidental contacts (e.g., brief shoul-
der/arm bumps). These cases are intentionally included as
challenging negatives because their spatial proximity and
short-term motion can resemble pre-fight cues and may lead
to false positives in real-time surveillance.
Together, the supplementary data increases contextual
relevance for educational scenarios while preserving the real-
world diversity provided by the original RWF-2000 subset.

IV. SYSTEMATIC BENCHMARK AND EVALUATION
METHOD
A. Overview of the Evaluation Framework
This study presents a systematic benchmarking framework
to evaluate deep learning models for school violence de-
tection, with a particular focus on the trade-off between
recognition accuracy and computational efficiency. Instead
of proposing new architectures, the framework aims to pro-
vide a fair and consistent comparison across representative
models by using a unified training and evaluation protocol,
while preserving each architecture’s canonical input config-
uration (e.g., default input resolution and preprocessing).
Two datasets, RWF-2000 and the school-oriented RWF-
2000+, are employed to assess both generalization and de-
ployment relevance. An overview of the evaluation pipeline,
covering training, testing, and edge deployment, is illustrated
in Fig. 2.
B. MoViNet-Stream Architecture and Variant Efficiency
Analysis
MoViNet (Mobile Video Network) [18] was selected as
a representative lightweight architecture due to its design
explicitly targeting efficient video recognition under resource
constraints. Compared to conventional 3D CNNs, MoViNet
introduces architectural features that are well aligned with
real-time school surveillance requirements:
• Streaming inference: temporal states are preserved
across frames, enabling continuous video processing
with low latency.
• Temporally separable convolutions: reduce computa-
tional cost while maintaining sensitivity to short-term
motion dynamics.
• Edge-aware design: optimized for deployment on em-
bedded platforms with limited compute resources.
To analyze the accuracy–efficiency trade-off, two repre-
sentative variants were evaluated. MoViNet-A0-Stream em-
phasizes inference speed and low resource consumption,
whereas MoViNet-A2-Stream provides increased representa-
tional capacity at higher computational cost. These variants
capture two ends of the efficiency spectrum relevant to real-
time surveillance systems.
C. Video Preprocessing and Input Configuration
All models take RGB frame sequences as input. Each
input video is represented as a fixed-length clip of con-
secutive frames to capture spatiotemporal dynamics relevant
to violent behaviors. For evaluation, clips are extracted as
contiguous frame sequences to preserve motion continuity
and ensure consistent clip-level measurement across models.
Input resolutions follow the canonical configuration of
each model architecture rather than enforcing a single uni-
form spatial resolution. Specifically, MoViNet-A0-Stream
adopts 172×172, MoViNet-A2-Stream uses 224×224, C3D-
based models use 112×112, and X3D-XS + CoAtNet-0 uses
224 × 224. For all models, the frame rate is fixed at 25 fps
to ensure consistent temporal sampling.
During training, clips are randomly sampled along the
temporal axis and basic geometric augmentations are applied
to improve generalization under varying surveillance condi-
tions. During testing, we disable random temporal sampling
and use a deterministic clip extraction strategy to ensure
reproducible comparisons.
D. Training Setup and Evaluation Protocol
All MoViNet-Stream models were initialized with pre-
trained weights from the Kinetics-600 dataset and fine-tuned
independently on the RWF-2000 and RWF-2000+ datasets
under a unified training protocol.
The task was formulated as a binary classification problem
(Fight vs. Non-Fight). Model performance was evaluated
using complementary metrics, where Top-1 accuracy reflects

C. Edge AI Deployment on NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin
To characterize on-device inference efficiency under em-
bedded constraints, we benchmarked MoViNet-A0-Stream
on an NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin (32GB LPDDR5, 8-core
Arm Cortex-A78AE CPU, and an Ampere GPU with Tensor
Cores). Unless otherwise noted, all runtime measurements
in this section were obtained directly on the Jetson platform.
MoViNet-A0-Stream was chosen for deployment evalua-
tion because it offers a favorable accuracy–efficiency trade-
off in our offline benchmark while incurring substantially
lower compute cost than larger variants. Given the higher
parameter count and GFLOPs of MoViNet-A2-Stream, its
throughput is expected to be lower on embedded hardware;
therefore, we report on-device results for A0 as the primary
deployment-oriented operating point.
For deployment, the model was exported to ONNX and
optimized with TensorRT using FP16 to leverage mixed-
precision acceleration on Jetson. As summarized in Table IV,
the PyTorch FP32 implementation requires 68.76 ms per clip
(14.54 clips/s), whereas TensorRT FP16 reduces latency to
14.08 ms per clip and increases throughput to 71.02 clips/s.
The corresponding accuracy changes modestly (91.99% vs.
90.05%) under the same evaluation setting. Overall, these
results show that TensorRT mixed-precision optimization can
markedly improve clip-level throughput for the evaluated
configuration on Jetson.
Finally, MoViNet’s streaming formulation maintains tem-
poral state across consecutive inputs, which can reduce
redundant computation relative to non-streaming 3D CNN
inference. While this deployment study focuses on clip-level
compute performance rather than full system latency (e.g.,

D. Case Study and Error Analysis
To further analyze the contribution of key design com-
ponents, a qualitative ablation-style analysis was conducted
to examine how different modeling factors affect the behav-
ior of the MoViNet-Stream architecture in realistic school
surveillance scenarios. Representative prediction cases are
shown in Figure 4. First, the impact of streaming-based tem-
poral modeling is observed by comparing predictions under
short, transient motions versus sustained motion patterns. In
false positive cases (Figure 4a), brief non-violent interactions
such as handshakes or close physical contact are occasion-
ally misclassified as violent. This behavior indicates that
emphasizing short-term temporal dynamics alone, without
sufficient contextual persistence, can cause the model to
overreact to rapid but benign movements.
Second, the effectiveness of temporal accumulation across
consecutive frames is evident in true positive examples
(Figure 4b). When violent actions exhibit consistent and
high-intensity motion over time, the streaming mechanism
enables the model to integrate temporal evidence progres-
sively, leading to stable and confident predictions. This ob-
servation highlights the importance of continuous temporal
state propagation in distinguishing genuine violence from
momentary interactions.
Third, the influence of training data diversity is reflected
in the reduction of false negatives when using the extended
RWF-2000+ dataset. Mild or short-lived confrontations that
were previously misclassified become easier to detect after
exposure to school-specific interaction patterns, suggesting
that contextual diversity plays a complementary role to
architectural efficiency.
VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
This study presented a deployment-aware evaluation of
deep learning models for school violence detection, em-
phasizing the trade-off between recognition accuracy and
computational efficiency under real-time constraints. Exper-
iments on RWF-2000 and the school-oriented RWF-2000+
dataset show that lightweight streaming architectures, partic-
ularly MoViNet-Stream variants, achieve a favorable balance
between accuracy and practical deployability compared with
conventional 3D CNN-based baselines. Edge-device mea-
surements on an NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin further indicate
that streaming inference, combined with mixed-precision
optimization, enables real-time on-device detection with low
computational overhead. Overall, our findings suggest that
accuracy-centric evaluation alone is insufficient for surveil-
lance applications, and that deployment-aware benchmarking
is essential for real-world school monitoring systems.
Future work will extend RWF-2000+ along two direc-
tions. First, we will incorporate a dedicated early-warning
evaluation protocol to quantify the minimum temporal ob-
servation window required for stable alerting (e.g., sub-
second triggering behavior) in streaming settings. Second,
we will expand the Non-Fight class with additional hard-
negative activities (e.g., high-motion actions and prolonged
close-contact interactions) to further reduce false alarms
under realistic school dynamics. Finally, we will explore
multimodal cues (e.g., pose or audio when available) to
improve robustness while maintaining real-time performance
on embedded devices.
